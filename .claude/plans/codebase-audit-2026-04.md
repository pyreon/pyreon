# Codebase audit — 2026-04-27

## Why this exists

Recent sessions have been adding code (perf optimizations, infra, RFCs) on top of an already-large surface area that **keeps producing real-app regressions**. Before more additions, this audit asks: what do we actually have, who uses it, what keeps breaking, what should we remove or consolidate?

Generated by `scripts/audit-codebase.ts` (also in this PR). Re-runnable.

For context: 61 packages × ~1,650 LOC each on average. That's a small framework's worth of code per **package**, not per project. Maintenance cost scales with surface area, not with usage — and right now we have a lot of code that nobody uses.

The right question for each package isn't "is this useful" but "does its consumer count justify its fix-rate". Sorted with that in mind.

**Combined: 5,170 LOC of source + 10,021 LOC of tests across 4 compat packages with zero internal use.** They exist as migration shims for users porting from those frameworks, but no example app validates them. Either:

## Recommendations — concrete, ordered by ROI

### High ROI (do first)

1. **Archive `@pyreon/dnd`.** 687 LOC of source, no internal consumers, 3 fix commits in 6 months for code that nobody imports. Move to a `archived/` directory or separate repo.
2. **Decide on the 4 compat packages.** ~5,170 LOC + ~10,021 test LOC. Either commit to validating each (real example app per shim, in the verify-modes matrix), or move out of the main repo. Not picking is the worst option — they keep accruing maintenance cost.
3. **Investigate the document 14MB lib.** Lazy-load story is documented (`Heavy renderers lazy-loaded (PDF ~300KB, DOCX ~100KB, XLSX ~500KB, PPTX ~200KB)`) but the lib total is 14MB. Either the lazy-loading isn't working in production builds or the doc is out of date. Either is bad.
4. **Investigate the charts 9MB lib.** Same question — ECharts tree-shaking story documented, 9MB landed.
5. **Update `CLAUDE.md`'s "fastest framework on all benchmarks" claim.** Honest version: "competitive with Solid on synthetic benchmarks, no real-app head-to-head yet."

### Medium ROI (do next)

6. **Audit `@pyreon/lint` rules for usage.** 59 rules, 6,915 LOC, 1 internal consumer. Find which rules fire in real apps (the showcases) and which haven't fired in 6 months. Drop the dead ones.
7. **Consider folding `@pyreon/connector-document` into `@pyreon/document-primitives`.** They exist as a bridge pair; the bridge has 4 consumers and the primitives 2. Often these split-package patterns become merge candidates once the original architectural reason fades.
8. **Decide on `@pyreon/feature`.** 1,060 LOC for 2 consumers. Either build a real example that proves the value, or de-prioritize until a real consumer asks.
9. **Audit `@pyreon/hooks` for unused hooks.** 35 hooks, 1,698 LOC. Find which hooks have 0 consumer references in examples — might be over-built.
10. **Consolidate `@pyreon/cli` and `@pyreon/zero-cli`.** Two standalone CLIs with overlapping scope. One is enough.

### Low ROI (consider later)

11. **Set bundle-size budgets per package.** Once measurements exist (we have `libKB` now), define ceilings. PRs that break the budget need justification.
12. **Set fix-rate thresholds.** A package with 20 fixes in 6 months is signaling something — either it's load-bearing (lint isn't), or under-designed. Triage the top-5 fix-rate packages quarterly.
13. **Add a "real app smoke" gate.** PRs to Tier A packages should pass a real-app smoke (bokisch.com or a clone). Synthetic tests ≠ real-app stability — the regressions in PRs #197, #200, #336, #349 all proved this.

## Aggregate insight

**Of 100,484 source LOC across 61 packages:**
- ~13,000 LOC (13%) is in the 5 high-churn foundation packages (Tier A) — that's where stability work pays the most.
- ~5,170 LOC (5%) is in compat shims with zero internal use — clearly removable.
- ~3,000 LOC is in clearly-marginal packages (dnd, feature, rx) — needs decision.
- ~6,915 LOC is in @pyreon/lint with 1 consumer and 20 fixes/6mo — needs scope decision.

**So roughly 15% of the codebase is paying maintenance cost without proportional value.** That's the slack — the audit's main finding.

## What this audit does NOT cover

- **Bundle size after tree-shake at consumer level.** We measure `lib/` but apps tree-shake. A 14MB lib might be 1MB in a real app build. Worth measuring properly.
- **Real-app regression coverage.** The 371 fix-rate counts commits that LANDED. It doesn't count regressions that the test suite missed and that a real app caught later.
- **Documentation completeness.** Some packages have `manifest.ts` (driving generated docs), some don't.
- **Test quality vs quantity.** A package with high test/src ratio could be over-mocked or under-real-shape. The mock-vnode audit (PR #311 area) hits this.

These are follow-up audits if needed.
